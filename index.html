The Open Rent Initiative
01 — The Problem

The challenge
with rent data

In Minnesota, structural gaps in rent data availability prevent an accurate assessment of housing market challenges. Current municipal records are unstandardized and limited in scope, federal surveys like the American Community Survey rely on infrequent updates and small samples that miss real-time localized changes, and high-quality commercial datasets remain behind cost-prohibitive paywalls.

01
Undercounting Affordable Stock
Single-family rentals and Naturally Occurring Affordable Housing (NOAH) are often invisible, hindering the state’s ability to identify preservation risks.
02
Reactive Posture
Without real-time tracking of rent hikes, vacancy rates, or neighborhood trends, stakeholders react only after displacement occurs.
03
Information Asymmetry
Well-resourced property owners access sophisticated analytics, while renters, advocates, and policymakers operate with poor data.
04
Operational Friction
Manually aggregating disparate sources is high-cost, and results lack the granularity to filter for transit corridors or school districts.

02 — The Solution

How we’re addressing
the challenge

Open Rent transforms fragmented rent data into accessible, actionable public infrastructure through three core capabilities.

01
Data Aggregation
Combines national, state, and local data from private, commercial, and government sources into a single inventory — subsidized units, NOAH, market-rate, and single-family rentals — layered with demographics, transit, and income data.
02
Interactive Tools
Open-source geospatial mapping with heatmaps, multi-layered views, and advanced filtering so non-technical users can visually track inventory and displacement risks.
03
Market Insights
Tools for monitoring rental inventory, tracking neighborhood-level rent stability, comparing across geographies, and measuring affordability and economic health.

04 — Who It Serves

Use cases

Use Case 01
Proactive Eviction Prevention
Housing Navigators & Service Providers
Monitor micro-neighborhoods for sudden rent hikes and proactively deploy emergency financial assistance and tenant education before families face displacement — especially critical for communities affected by operations like Metro Surge targeting immigrant households.
Use Case 02
Strategic Investment & Preservation
Program Officers & Housing Developers
Overlay publicly-funded housing maps with real-time rental rates and neighborhood amenity data to instantly identify NOAH properties at highest risk of market-rate conversion.
Use Case 03
Data-Driven Policy Advocacy
Advocates & Civic Leaders
Generate localized dashboards showing rent trajectories vs. wage growth in specific legislative districts, bringing data-backed arguments to city council and state legislative hearings to drive systemic policy change.

06 — Our Approach

How we’re building
in Minnesota

Open Rent Minnesota draws on national data infrastructure (Dwellsy, Zillow, ACS), state data (affordably financed property records), and municipal sources (fire occupancy, parcel records) — guided by expertise at three levels.

Cross-Regional Insights
Sharing learnings on innovative data-sourcing and predictive modeling strategies developed in the San Francisco Bay Area, supported by the San Francisco Foundation and the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative.
Local Partnership
Close partnership and co-ownership with HousingLink to integrate deep institutional knowledge of the Minnesota rental market directly into our data pipelines.
Local Expert Guidance
A working group of senior research leaders from Minnesota Housing, the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis, Hennepin County, City of Minneapolis, City of St. Paul, and Metropolitan Council.
Case Study: St. Paul Rent Stabilization Ordinance
To map units subject to St. Paul’s rent stabilization policy, Open Rent merged Ramsey County Parcels, Fire Inspection Certificate of Occupancy records, and HousingLink Streams data using geospatial and fuzzy matching — and employed a random forest model to predict rental status with 98% accuracy. Result: ~63,000 rental units identified out of ~135,600 total residential units, with ~24,000 rentals (38%) flagged as likely qualifying for exemptions.
